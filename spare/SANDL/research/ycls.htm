While many studies have shown an association between economic deprivation and impaired cognitive development in childhood, no studies have explored the impact of economic and environmental disadvantage on more fundamental developmental capacities such as neurocognitive executive control functions. The Young Children’s Learning Study proposes that one mechanism for the impact of poverty on cognitive development may be through a delay or impairment in inhibitory executive control functions. As such, research is focused on the developmental trajectories of component cognitive abilities within inhibition and executive control functions in preschool to early school aged children. In particular, we focus on functions that include inhibitions in both attention and action. Additionally, we also investigate environmental conditions that might moderate the potential impact of economic adversity on children’s development. More specifically, parental education, parental behavior with the child, parental beliefs and attitudes, parent’s mental health, as well as the stability and stimulating richness of the home environment may all determine how much economic disadvantage does or does not signal experiential poverty in the child’s developmental landscape.
