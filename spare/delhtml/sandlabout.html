Kids Corner

The Social Affective Neuroscience and Development Laboratory is part of the Child Study Center at the Yale School of Medicine. The lab draws on the resources of the Developmental Electrophysiology Laboratory for ongoing studies of fear and anxiety in children and adults. Broadly speaking, our work touches upon several areas including anxiety-stress relationships, fear regulatory systems, developmental changes and comorbidity. For several years now we have used the air-puff threat paradigm developed by Christian Grillon to so study fear potentiated startle and fear extinction. One example of this work is underway in collaboration with Doug Mennin of Yale’s Psychology Department. With Dr. Mennin we are examining fear potentiated startle and EEG in conjunction with a yoked attention cuing paradigm among high and low trait worriers (adult). We expect that high trait worry individuals will have difficulty disengaging attention from threatening stimuli. A second example of our science involves a typically developing cohort of children 8-12 yrs. whom we assessed on a chronometric version of the startle threat paradigm. With this paradigm we used growth curve modeling to understand rate of change in the startle response during threat and safety cues. Importantly we’ve found that safety signals are worthy of study in and of themselves. Some children appear to be exquisitely sensitive to the removal of a safety cue. Given the widely held belief that safety signals can interfere with the progress of exposure therapy, we believe this work could shed some light on how safety signals function in the context fear extinction. Lastly, we are interested in the role anxiety plays in as a comorbid clinical problem among children with oppositional behavior problems. We were recently funded through a NARSAD young investigator award to conduct this research.
